# Tax Comparison: Optimized C-Corp in Practice

The common objection to C-Corp status is double taxation. This section shows what the actual tax burden looks like when proper C-Corp optimization strategies are applied.

**Important:** These are illustrative examples using simplified assumptions. Actual tax calculations would depend on Sahil's complete income picture, state tax considerations, and specific plan designs. This is meant to demonstrate the framework, not to produce filing-ready numbers.

---

## Scenario: $5M in Pre-Tax Corporate Earnings

Starting point: Gumroad generates $5M in pre-tax earnings available for compensation, retention, and distribution.

### Step 1: Salary to Sahil

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Salary (W-2 from Gumroad) | $400,000 |
| Federal income tax (est. 37% marginal bracket) | ~$120,000 |
| Employee FICA (Social Security + Medicare) | ~$21,000 |
| Additional Medicare tax (0.9% over $200K) | ~$1,800 |
| State income tax (varies) | ~$20,000 |
| **Total tax on salary** | **~$163,000** |
| **Sahil's after-tax from salary** | **~$237,000** |

The salary is fully deductible by Gumroad, reducing corporate taxable income by $400,000. Gumroad also pays employer FICA (~$21,000), which is also deductible.

Note: Sahil also receives W-2 income from the IRS. His actual marginal rates depend on the combined income. This illustration assumes the Gumroad salary is in the top bracket.

### Step 2: Retirement Plan Contributions

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Defined benefit plan contribution | ~$175,000 |
| 401(k) employer contribution | ~$75,000 |
| **Total retirement contributions** | **$250,000** |
| **Tax on contributions this year** | **$0** |

These contributions are tax-deductible to the corporation and not currently taxable to Sahil. The money grows tax-deferred until withdrawal in retirement.

Corporate taxable income is reduced by another $250,000.

### Step 3: Corporate Tax on Remaining Income

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Pre-tax earnings | $5,000,000 |
| Less: Salary + employer FICA | ($421,000) |
| Less: Retirement contributions | ($250,000) |
| Less: Other operating deductions | (assumed net) |
| **Corporate taxable income** | **~$4,329,000** |
| Federal corporate tax (21%) | **$908,000** |
| State corporate tax (varies, ~1-2%) | ~$50,000 |
| **Total corporate tax** | **~$958,000** |

After corporate tax, Gumroad has approximately $3,371,000 available.

### Step 4: Retained Earnings (Business Purpose)

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Retained for business purposes | $2,000,000 |
| Tax on retention | $0 (already taxed at corporate level) |

This $2M stays in the corporation. It has already been taxed at 21%. No additional tax is due as long as the retention serves a documented business purpose (see accumulated earnings tax defense).

The remaining amount available for dividends is approximately $1,371,000. However, the charter requires 60% of net income to be distributed. Let's adjust:

### Step 4 (Adjusted): Following the 60% Dividend Charter

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Net income (after corporate tax) | ~$3,371,000 |
| 60% dividend requirement | ~$2,023,000 |
| Retained (40%) | ~$1,348,000 |

### Step 5: Qualified Dividend Distribution

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Qualified dividends to Sahil (majority share, est.) | ~$1,440,000 |
| Federal tax on qualified dividends (20%) | $288,000 |
| Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%) | $54,720 |
| **Total tax on dividends** | **~$342,720** |
| **Sahil's after-tax from dividends** | **~$1,097,280** |

### Total Tax Summary

| Tax Component | Amount |
|---------------|--------|
| Sahil's income tax on salary | $163,000 |
| Corporate income tax | $958,000 |
| Sahil's tax on dividends | $342,720 |
| **Total taxes paid** | **~$1,463,720** |

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Pre-tax corporate earnings | $5,000,000 |
| Total taxes paid | ~$1,464,000 |
| **Effective tax rate** | **~29.3%** |

### With R&D Tax Credits

| Adjustment | Amount |
|------------|--------|
| R&D credit (estimated) | ($100,000) |
| **Adjusted total taxes** | **~$1,364,000** |
| **Adjusted effective rate** | **~27.3%** |

## What Does This Compare To?

### Hypothetical S-Corp (If It Were Possible)

If Gumroad were an S-Corp and passed $5M through to Sahil:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Pass-through income to Sahil | $5,000,000 |
| Federal income tax (37% marginal) | ~$1,700,000 |
| State income tax | ~$250,000 |
| Net Investment Income Tax / Additional Medicare | ~$50,000 |
| **Total tax** | **~$2,000,000** |
| **Effective rate** | **~40%** |

S-Corp pass-through would result in all income being taxed at ordinary rates (up to 37% federal), because Gumroad's income does not qualify for the Section 199A deduction (which is limited for specified service trades and phases out above income thresholds).

**The optimized C-Corp saves roughly $500,000-$600,000 per year compared to a hypothetical S-Corp pass-through** in this scenario.

### Unoptimized C-Corp

Without planning -- just paying corporate tax and distributing the rest:

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Corporate tax on $5M (21%) | $1,050,000 |
| After-tax available | $3,950,000 |
| Dividend tax on full distribution (23.8%) | $940,100 |
| **Total tax** | **~$1,990,000** |
| **Effective rate** | **~39.8%** |

The unoptimized C-Corp is almost identical to the S-Corp pass-through in total tax burden. The optimization strategies save approximately **$525,000 per year** compared to the naive approach.

## Key Takeaways

### 1. The "Double Tax" Is Real but Manageable

Yes, C-Corp income is taxed twice. But:
- The corporate rate is only 21%
- Qualified dividends are taxed at 23.8%, not 37%
- Salary and retirement contributions are deductible at the corporate level, eliminating one layer of tax on those amounts
- R&D credits directly reduce corporate tax liability

### 2. C-Corp Can Be Cheaper Than S-Corp

When you account for:
- Qualified dividend rates vs. ordinary income rates
- Retirement plan deduction capacity
- R&D credits
- The ability to retain earnings at 21% (vs. pass-through at 37%)
- QSBS eligibility (worth $2.38M in a future exit)

The C-Corp is the superior structure for Gumroad by a significant margin.

### 3. The Real Win Is QSBS

Even if the annual tax burden were slightly higher as a C-Corp (it is not, with proper planning), the $2.38M QSBS benefit dwarfs any annual difference. Over a 10-year holding period, you would need the S-Corp to save more than $238,000 per year in operating taxes just to break even on the QSBS destruction -- and as shown above, the S-Corp actually costs more.

### 4. Planning Matters More Than Structure

The difference between an unoptimized C-Corp (~40% effective rate) and an optimized one (~27% effective rate) is larger than the difference between an S-Corp and a C-Corp. Structure gets all the attention, but execution is where the money is.

## What This Does Not Include

This illustration is simplified. A complete analysis would also consider:

- State and local taxes (which vary significantly by jurisdiction)
- Sahil's IRS W-2 income and its interaction with Gumroad income
- Alternative Minimum Tax considerations
- Net Investment Income Tax optimization
- Timing strategies (deferral, acceleration of income/deductions)
- Estate and gift tax planning at $100M+ valuation
- The impact of Crowd SAFE conversion on shareholder-level calculations
