# Corporate History

Gumroad's history is essential context for its tax and corporate structure analysis. The company's journey from venture-backed startup to near-death to profitable creator platform shapes every recommendation in this document.

---

## Timeline

### 2011 -- Founded

Sahil Lavingia founds Gumroad at age 19 after leaving Pinterest, where he was the second employee. The vision: make it as easy to sell a product as it is to share a link.

Incorporated as a **Delaware C-Corporation** -- the standard structure for a company planning to raise venture capital.

### 2012 -- Seed & Series A

**Seed round: $1.1M** from an exceptional group of angel investors and funds:
- Max Levchin (PayPal co-founder)
- Chris Sacca (Lowercase Capital)
- Ron Conway (SV Angel)
- Naval Ravikant (AngelList)
- Accel Partners
- First Round Capital
- Collaborative Fund

**Series A: $7M** led by Kleiner Perkins Caufield & Byers (KPCB), with Mike Abbott as the lead partner. Pre-money valuation approximately $30M.

At this point, Gumroad has a classic Silicon Valley cap table: founder equity, angel investors, seed funds, and a top-tier lead VC with preferred stock and liquidation preferences.

### 2013-2014 -- Growth and Peak Ambition

Gumroad grows rapidly. The team scales. The plan is the standard venture playbook: raise a Series B, keep growing, aim for a large outcome.

**November 2014:** Growth stalls. The metrics stop going up and to the right.

### 2015 -- The Near-Death

**Series B fundraising attempt fails.** No firm leads a round.

**June 2015:** The numbers tell the story:
- Monthly revenue: $89,000
- Monthly net loss: -$351,000
- Burn rate is unsustainable
- Runway is measured in months

Sahil makes the hardest decision a founder can make: **75% of the team is laid off.** Headcount drops from 20 to 5.

The company enters survival mode.

### 2016-2017 -- The Rebuild

Sahil rebuilds Gumroad around a fundamentally different model. Instead of a venture-scale team chasing growth, the company becomes a small, profitable operation run by contractors.

**June 2016:** Gumroad is profitable for the first time on a monthly basis.

**November 2017:** The single most important structural event in Gumroad's history. **Kleiner Perkins sells its entire stake back to Gumroad for $1.**

This is extraordinary. KPCB had led a $7M Series A and held significant preferred stock with liquidation preferences. By selling for $1, total outstanding liquidation preferences drop from approximately **$16.5M to $2.5M** (the remaining seed/Series A investors who did not exit).

This event dramatically simplifies the cap table and makes future exits far more favorable for common shareholders -- primarily Sahil.

### 2018-2020 -- Quiet Growth

Gumroad grows steadily as a profitable, bootstrapped-style business. No new funding. Small team. Sahil writes openly about the journey, building a significant personal following.

The creator economy begins its massive expansion. Gumroad is well-positioned.

### March 2021 -- The Crowdfunding Moment

Gumroad raises capital through two simultaneous rounds:

**Republic Crowdfunding (Regulation CF):**
- **$5M+ raised from 7,387 individual investors**
- Structure: Crowd SAFE (Simple Agreement for Future Equity)
- Valuation cap: $100M
- Discount: 20%
- Hit the fundraising cap in **one day**

**Private round:**
- $1M+ from Naval Ravikant and Jason Fried (Basecamp/37signals)
- Valuation: $125M

This raise is transformative for two reasons:

1. It gives Gumroad growth capital without traditional VC dynamics
2. It creates a shareholder base of 7,387+ people, which has profound implications for corporate structure (as detailed in the S-Corp analysis)

**Peak GMV in 2021: ~$185M**, driven by the pandemic-era creator economy boom.

### Early 2023 -- The Pricing Pivot

Gumroad changes its pricing model to a **flat 10% take rate** on all transactions, replacing a tiered structure. This is controversial with some creators but dramatically increases revenue.

**Monthly revenue jumps from approximately $1M to $1.8M.** Annual revenue roughly doubles from $11M (2022) to $21M (2023).

### July 2023 -- First Dividend

Gumroad distributes a **$1M trial dividend** through the Flexile platform. This is the first cash return to shareholders in the company's history.

### May 2024 -- Full Annual Dividend

**$5.34M distributed** -- representing 60% of 2023 net income, per the charter provision. This establishes the ongoing dividend policy.

### March-May 2025 -- DOGE

Sahil volunteers with the Department of Government Efficiency (DOGE) initiative at the Department of Veterans Affairs. He is terminated after 55 days.

### November 2025 -- The IRS

Sahil joins the Internal Revenue Service as a **career employee** (not a political appointee), publicly stating his intention to serve for 10 years. He describes the motivation as wanting to help modernize the agency's technology.

This creates an unusual personal tax situation: W-2 income from the IRS, dividend income from Gumroad, and potentially other compensation from Gumroad as well -- all of which need coordinated planning.

## Why This History Matters for Tax Planning

Every major event in Gumroad's history has tax implications:

| Event | Tax Implication |
|-------|-----------------|
| Delaware C-Corp incorporation (2011) | QSBS clock starts for Sahil's founder stock |
| KPCB sells for $1 (2017) | Liquidation preferences drop, simplifying exit math |
| 7,387 crowdfunding investors (2021) | S-Corp conversion becomes impossible |
| Crowd SAFE structure (2021) | QSBS 5-year clock for investors starts at conversion, not investment |
| Pricing change doubles revenue (2023) | Higher income magnifies both tax liability and planning opportunities |
| 60% dividend policy (2023-24) | Creates accumulated earnings tax questions on the 40% retained |
| IRS employment (2025) | Multi-source income requiring coordinated planning |
